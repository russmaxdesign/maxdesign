Understanding screen reader speech queues

Published: 6 December 2025

To understand how screen readers work — and to diagnose issues — you need to understand the screen reader speech queue.

But before we look at that queue, we need to look at the thing that enters it: the utterance.

What is an utterance?

An utterance is the unit of speech that screen readers prepare to output — sometimes a sentence, sometimes a phrase, sometimes only a token like a single character.

  • Focus changes
  • AXEvents from the browser
  • Live regions
  • User actions, such as reading commands

An utterance is not spoken immediately. It is added to the speech queue to be processed.

For this analogy, think of utterances as different types of vehicles:

  • Focus events are like ambulances, almost always go to the high-priority lane.
  • Name and value changes are like ordinary cars.
  • Typing events (individual keystrokes) are like motor scooters.
  • Live region updates are like delivery vans.
  • User-initiated commands (explicit user intent) are like buses that carry priority passengers.

What is the speech queue?

In this analogy, the speech queue is like a traffic intersection with two lanes:

  1. A high-priority lane for focus and alerts
  2. A low-priority lane for polite live regions and typing

More importantly, the speech queue has a traffic inspector. This inspector checks every vehicle before letting it through.

The inspector may decide to:

  • Speak it
  • Delay it
  • Merge it
  • Interrupt something else
  • Ignore it

Scenarios

Interrupt

  • An alert interrupts anything currently speaking.
  • Alerts travel through the high-priority lane.

Speak immediately

  • Focus moves to a button and the screen reader says “Button, Search”.
  • Focus events always use the high-priority lane.

User commands

  • The user presses a screen reader command, such as “read next line” or “read current item”.
  • These commands act like buses carrying priority passengers.
  • They jump straight to the front of the high-priority lane and interrupt anything being spoken.

Delay or batch

  • A polite live region is spoken later when the screen reader is idle.
  • These are cars in the low-priority lane that wait their turn.

Ignore

  • Repeated small updates such as rapid changes in a low-priority lane may be ignored.
  • These are low-value cars that the inspector pushes aside.

Merge events

  • A user types quickly and the screen reader only speaks the final letter or speaks nothing.
  • These are motor scooters arriving almost all at once in the low-priority lane.
  • There may be too many scooters, arriving too fast.
  • The traffic inspector becomes overwhelmed and waves them all through, only registering the last one. In reality, it’s not actual ‘overload’ — it’s intentional debouncing.

Screen readers behave differently

Important: The traffic analogy in this article is only a teaching tool.

Real screen readers do not follow the same rules, and they do not handle speech output in exactly the same way.

Each screen reader has:

  • its own speech queue implementation
  • its own priority system
  • its own timing logic
  • its own internal heuristics and strategies

Because of this, the same utterance may be:

  • spoken immediately in one screen reader
  • delayed in another
  • merged into a single announcement
  • ignored entirely

This is expected, normal, and part of how each product is designed.

Why this queue matters for accessibility

Once you understand utterances and how they move through the speech queue, you will avoid misinterpreting screen reader behaviour.

You are much less likely to wonder why something is spoken immediately, spoken after a pause, merged with something else, or not spoken at all.

Stay safe in traffic!
