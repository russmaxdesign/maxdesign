What does “hidden” actually mean in Accessible Name computation?

Published: 8 December 2025

In other words: when is an element allowed to contribute to an accessible name or description, and when is it excluded — except when intentionally referenced using aria-labelledby or aria-describedby?

There are four categories:

1. display:none, visibility:hidden, visibility:collapse, content-visibility:hidden

These ARE considered hidden.

These elements are not rendered, do not take up space, and do not appear in the accessibility tree.

(Note: behaviour of visibility:collapse varies for table rows/columns, but for non-table elements it behaves the same as visibility:hidden.)

Verdict: They cannot contribute to accessible names or descriptions.

2. opacity:0, filter:opacity(0%), or similar SVG transparency techniques

These are NOT considered hidden.

Even though the element is visually invisible, it is still present in the layout, in the DOM, in the accessibility tree, and as selectable or copyable text.

Verdict: They can contribute to accessible names and descriptions.

3. aria-hidden="true"

This IS considered hidden.

The author has explicitly instructed the browser to exclude the element from accessibility APIs. It is removed from the accessibility tree.

Verdict: It cannot contribute to accessible names or descriptions.

4. Positioned off-screen or visually covered by other content

These are NOT considered hidden.

If an element is moved far off-screen or visually obscured by another element, it still exists in the DOM and in the accessibility tree. This includes patterns like “visually hidden but accessible” text used for contextual labelling.

Verdict: They can contribute to accessible names and descriptions.

Keep in mind

Hidden content is normally ignored, but aria-labelledby and aria-describedby can reference hidden elements and use them to create accessible names or descriptions for other elements.
